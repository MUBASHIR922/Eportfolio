Reflective Writing

This reflective piece evaluates my learning journey across the Big Data and Database Management Module, Which formed the practical foundation of the overall 12 unit structure. At the beginning of the module, my understanding of big data concepts existed largely at a conceptual level. While I had previously used SQL and databases in a practical sense, my exposure was fragmented and lacked depth, practical sense, particularly in relation to integrating multiple datasets and designing robust relational databases. My initial expectation was that the module would remain largely theoretical; however, as the units progressed, it became increasingly applied and industry-oriented.

Unit 1 to 3 introduced core big data concepts data types, format, and data collection mechanism. These units clarified the distinctions between structured and highlighted the importance of storage decisions in large-scale data systems. Unit 4 and 5 marked a significant shift towards practice, focusing on data cleaning, transformation and automation. Here, I engaged directly with messy, real-world datasets and encountered common issues such as inconsistent data types, missing values, and formatting errors. Unit 6 represented a pivotal stage in the module, where I designed and implemented a normalized PostgreSQL database, applying first to third normal form(1NF-3NF), defining primary and foreign keys, and resolving data integrity constraints.

Throughout this process, I polished my skills, troubleshooting issues related to joins, foreign key violation, and data type mismatches, particularly during ETL operations. Successfully resolving these issues transformed my understanding of databases from simple data storage systems into structured, rule- driven environments that support scalable and reliable analytics.

The most significant aspect of this learning experience was the transition from surface-level SQL usage to a deeper appreciation of database design principles and data integrity. Initially, task such as joining multiple tables and normalizing data appeared straight forward in theory but proved challenging in practice. Normalisation, in particular, required disciplined approach to separate entities, removing redundancy, and understanding functional dependencies. Applying 1NF to 3NF forced me to think critically about how data relationships impact query performance and long term maintainability.

Data cleaning and transformation further reinforced the realities of industry level data handling. Encountering frequent data type mismatches, especially with dates and Boolean fields, required systematic debugging and careful resolving them was highly motivating and significantly increased my confidence. This process strengthened my problem-solving mindset, as I learned to diagnose errors methodically rather than relying on trial-and-error approaches.

From a theoretical perspective, the module implicitly reinforced principles aligned with relational database theory and best practices in data engineering. The emphasis on staging tables ETL sequencing, and constraint management mirrored real-world workflows, bridging the gap between academic learning and professional application. Importantly, the experience highlighted that data quality and structure are prerequisites for meaningful analytics, a realization that reshaped how I perceive the role of databases within the broader big data ecosystem.

Critically reflecting on my approach, I recognise that earlier schema planning and more rigorous validation at initial stages could have reduced downstream issues. This insight demonstrates a maturation in my analytical thinking, moving beyond task completion towards optimisation and best practice alignment.

Moving forward, this module has clearly influenced how I intend to approach both academia projects and professional data roles. I now prioritise schema design, documentation, validation before implementation, recognizing their importance in preventing scalability and integrity issues. The confidence gained in SQL and PostgreSQL, and relational modelling positions me well for industry level analytics tasks, particularly those involving complex joins, ETL pipelines and database optimisation.

In alignment with the future of big data analytics outlined in Unit 12, this module has reinforced the necessity of strong data management fundamentals in an increasingly automated and data- driven landscape. As a Master’s level data science student, I now view database design not as a supporting skill but as a core competency underpinning ethical, scalable, and effective analytics. This reflective process confirms that the module has significantly contributed to both my technical competence and my professional readiness within the data science field.
